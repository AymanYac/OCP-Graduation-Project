FERTILIZERS  MARKET OVERVIEW

 

Fertilizers are most commonly used to improve soil fertility by enhancing nutrient content in the soil and, accordingly, to increase crop production and yields. Fertilizers can be categorized into macronutrients - which are needed in large quantities - and micronutrients. The primary macronutrients include nitrogen, phosphorus and potassium, which are essential elements required for plant growth.

Each of the three key nutrients serves a different vital function in plant formation and development, and a proper balance of the three nutrients is necessary to maximizing the fertilizer’s effectiveness. Phosphorus is essential to plant root development and is required for photosynthesis and seed germination. Nitrogen is an important determinant of plant growth and crop yield because it is a building block of protein and chlorophyll. Potassium improves a plant's water regime and its ability to withstand the stress of drought, disease, cold weather, weeds and insects. Although these nutrients are naturally found in soil, they are depleted over time by farming, which leads to declines in crop yields and land productivity. To replenish these nutrients, farmers must apply fertilizers.

In 2013, the global production of primary nutrients was estimated to amount to 182 million tons of nutrients, of which nitrogen was 61%, phosphorus was 23% and potash was 16%, according to the International Fertilizer Association (IFA).

 

Nitrogen

Nitrogen (“N”) increases yield by promoting protein formation. The primary input for producing nitrogen fertilizers is natural gas, which typically represents 70% to 90% of the cost to make ammonia. The two most common nitrogen fertilizer products urea and ammonium nitrates. Nitrogen fertilizer products have historically been subject to price volatility and natural gas input pricing and availability, contributing to significant swings in profitability.

Phosphorus

Phosphorus (“P”) is vital for root development and plays a key role in the photosynthesis process (i.e., the production, transportation and accumulation of sugars in the plant). Phosphorus is also involved in seed germination. The principal mineral used in the production of phosphorus-based fertilizers (phosphates) is phosphate rock, which is mined and then processed using sulphuric acid and ammonia. The primary phosphate fertilizer products are DAP, which has a phosphate content of 46% and a nitrogen content of 18%, and MAP, which has a phosphate content of 52% and a nitrogen content of 11%. The leading producing regions of phosphate fertilizers are Morocco, U.S., China and Russia, primarily because they contain the vast majority of global phosphate rock deposits.

The demand for phosphate fertilizers is increasing due to rising food needs of the world population, increased use of biofuels and the limitation of arable land. All these basic and structural needs and involve intensification of agricultural production on available surfaces. This increase yields through fertilizer application and should be responsible for the increase in agricultural production over the next decade.

Potassium

Potassium (“K”) helps regulate plants' physiological functions: it helps plants use water efficiently and improves plant durability, providing crops with protection from drought, disease, parasites and cold weather. Potassium is found in nature in the form of potash ore (KCI). Potash is mined either from underground mines or, less frequently, from naturally occurring surface or sub- surface brines. Potash does not require additional chemical conversion to be used as a plant nutrient. Naturally occurring, economically recoverable deposits of potash are scarce, deep in the earth and geographically concentrated.

 

PHOSPHATE-BASED PRODUCTS

SOURCES OF PHOSPHATE

The phosphorus element is a component of living cells of humans being. In vertebrates, it appears in the bones and teeth. In plants, it is the one who carries the power produced by photosynthesis. Phosphate (P2O5 phosphorus pentoxide) is the most common form of the phosphorus element. Phosphorus is so essential to life.
In its natural state, it is in many forms. The vast majority of the world's phosphate reserves are of different origins including:

Sedimentary: Phosphate comes from sedimentation and fossilization of aquatic vertebrates and corals. Sedimentary phosphate mines are present in many part of the world but are economically exploitable mainly in Florida, the Mediterranean, China and Peru.

 

Volcanic: phosphate mining can also be found in the volcanic state: it is the case of South Africa phosphate or Russia. These mineral resources are disparate in term of quality and accessibility.

 

Agriculture: Global phosphate can also be derived from agricultural residues ("trash" cereal, animal wastes). Its use is mainly artisanal and non-industrialized way.

 

PHOSPHATE USES

Phosphate (any category: rock, phosphoric acid, fertilizers, etc.) is used 85% to produce fertilizers. However, it has many others uses such as production of animals feeds (nutrition for animals) as well as various industrial uses, including detergents and food usage such as sodas.

 

The primary end markets for phosphate-based products are as follows:

  • Phosphate rock: phosphate fertilizer producers for direct application on acidic soil and fertilizer producers or dealers that custom mix fertilizers;
  • Phosphoric acid: dealers that custom mix fertilizers and fertigation (i.e., the application of fertilizers through an irrigation system);
  • Purified phosphoric acid and feed phosphate products: producers of poultry, cattle and swine feed supplements and producers of food and beverage products, metal treatment, detergents and electronics; and
  • Solid fertilizers: dealers for direct application or custom mix fertilizers.

 

FERTILIZERS PHOSPHATE

Phosphate fertilizers are derived from the active ingredient of rock phosphate, P2O5. The rock contains a certain content of P2O5 may vary significantly depending on the site or in the layers of a deposit. The rock is converted into phosphoric acid, by the chemical reaction of phosphate rock with Sulphur. Phosphoric acid is also known as Merchant Grade Acid (MGA) when it meets a 54% P2O5 content. The phosphate can be applied directly in the ground in some cases.

The main solid fertilizers obtained from phosphoric acid are the TSP (Triple Super Phosphate), DAP (Di Ammonium Phosphate), MAP (Mono Ammonium Phosphate), the SSP (Single superphosphate), NPK and specialty fertilizers. The specialty fertilizers are obtained possibly adding other micro-nutrients, trace minerals and other supplements.

Fundamentals of phosphate-based products’ demand

Phosphate fertilizers demand are driven primarily by food, feed and fuel demand (which in turn are driven by, among other factors, population growth, a reduction in arable land, dietary changes in the developing world and increased bio-fuel consumption). Given the constraints on increasing the quantity of arable land available for cultivation, fertilizers are an important tool to improve agricultural yields and address the growing imbalance between food demand and supply.

Population Growth and Arable Land per Capita

Population growth is the primary driver of fertilizer demand. As the world’s population grows, urbanizes and industrializes, farm land per capita decreases and increased food production is required from each acre of farm land, which in turn requires more plant nutrients. The charts below reflect the forecast increase in world population and decline in global arable land per capita through 2050, both of which are long term drivers for global fertilizer demand.

 

In light of the expected growth of the world’s population and the expected reduction of available arable land stemming in part from increased urbanization and industrialization, the area of arable land per capita is expected to decrease by 21% globally between 2010 and 2050, according to the FAO. As a result of the limited ability to expand the existing stock of arable land, it will be necessary to increase yields on the existing fields to improve crop yields and meet future anticipated demand for food, which in turn is expected to increase demand for fertilizers, according to the FAO.

 

Dietary Changes in the Developing World

Developed nations currently use phosphate as a fertilizer more intensively than developing nations, but sustained economic growth in emerging markets is increasing food and feed demand and phosphate fertilizer use. Due to the growth in GDP, populations in emerging markets are shifting to more protein-rich diets as incomes increase, leading to increasing grain consumption for animal feed. The production of meat requires a significant amount of grain to feed farmed animals. For instance, it takes up to 7 pounds of grain to produce 1 pound of beef and 4 pounds of grain for 1 pound of pork.

 

According to the FAO, world meat consumption is projected to grow by 1.8% per year on average until 2021, while emerging market countries meat consumption is projected to grow by 2.1% per year on average during the same period.

 

Biofuel Demand Growth

Given increasing legislation on using alternatives to fossil fuels, biofuel production has increased substantially in recent years. This trend is affecting the agricultural industry significantly through its increasing demand for grain crops (such as corn in the United States, sugarcane in Brazil or sugar beet in the EU), which as a result has increased fertilizer demand. The United States is one of the major producers of bio fuels and according to the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (“EPA”), in 2014, 18.2 billion gallons of biofuel per year are legally required to be consumed to meet the renewable fuel standard as amended by the Energy Independence and Security Act (EISA), more than two times the amount in 2008.

SUPPLLY FUNDAMENTALS OF PHOSPHATES

 

Commercial phosphate fertilizers used in agriculture are typically made using three key inputs: phosphate rock, sulphur and ammonia. Phosphate rock is currently widely viewed as the only commercially viable source of phosphorous available globally. Typically found in areas of oceanic upwelling with a low energy environment, phosphate rock formations primarily occur as sedimentary marine phosphorites. The largest deposits of phosphate rock are found in Morocco, China, the Middle East, and the United States. Large igneous deposits have also been delineated in Brazil, Canada, Finland, Russia and South Africa. According to the USGS, global reserves of phosphate rock currently stand at approximately 67 billion tons, with Morocco (including Moroccan Sahara) contributing 50 billion tons, as shown in the world map below.

 

Phosphate rock is the key raw material used in the production of phosphate fertilizers, which is produced using sulphuric acid and ammonia.

The largest six phosphate rock country producers, which accounted for approximately 70% of the global trade market for phosphate rock, phosphoric acid and phosphate-based fertilizers in 2012, are Morocco, China, USA, Russia, Jordan and Egypt, as shown in the graph below.

 

The largest three phosphate rock country producers—Morocco, China and the United States—hold approximately 50% of the global trade market for phosphate rock, phosphoric acid and phosphate based fertilizers in 2012, according to IFA. In the phosphoric acid market and the phosphate-based fertilizer market, the largest three country producers held approximately 61% and 54% of global trade market share in 2012, according to the IFA. In 2012, according to IFA, the Group represented approximately 14%, 9% and 7% of the global phosphate rock, phosphoric acid and fertilizers production, respectively.

 

According to CRU, the production of phosphate rock has increased significantly since 2000, increasing from approximately 145.7 million tons in 2000 to approximately 195 million tons in 2012; over this period, production increased at a CAGR of 2.4%.

 

According to CRU, the production of phosphate-based fertilizers (DAP/MAP/TSP) has increased at healthy rate since 2000, increasing from approximately 20.1 million tons P2O5 in 2000 to approximately 30.7 million tons product by 2012; over this period, production increased at a CAGR of 3.6%.

 

Operating rates have also displayed resilience, being between 58% and 69% over the last decade. Over the 2000-2012 period, consumption increased at a CAGR of 3.6% while supply increased at a CAGR of 3.6%, according to CRU.

 

 

A number of brownfield and Greenfield projects have been contemplated through 2017, with the majority of the capacity increases expected in Morocco (OCP) and Saudi Arabia (Ma’aden). Barriers to entry to the phosphate industry include know-how, access to cost-competitive reserves and sufficient financial resources (which make brownfield projects attractive for capacity expansion).
